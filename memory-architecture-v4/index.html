This document is the view from the top — an as-built map of how conventions, facts, durable knowledge, and raw observations compose into something a future reader (including future-Claude arriving with no context) can orient in under five minutes. Read it the way you would read a field guide: begin at the hierarchy, follow the pipeline, check the skills, then the promotion paths, then the record of how we got here.

I

The Authority Hierarchy

Four layers, ranked. Disagreement is a promotion signal.

When two layers disagree about a fact or a convention, the higher-ranked layer wins — but the conflict itself is the point. It tells you the lower layer is stale, or that the convention needs clarifying, or that a one-off exception ought to be written down. Authority isn't just ordering. It is the engine of the system's self-correction.

Layers are arranged below by permanence and deliberation: edited slowly at the top, harvested automatically at the bottom. Bright is loud. Dim is raw. Hover a stratum to open it.

Read as Geological strata. Older and heavier above, faster and noisier below. A fact buried at tier 4 earns its way up by proving useful across sessions.

Disagreement is a promotion signal

A conflict between layers does not mean "pick the right one and move on." It means the lower layer is asking to be lifted: the fact is worth promoting, the exception worth documenting, the convention worth clarifying. The system self-corrects because authority is ranked.

The rule, stated once

CLAUDE.md > auto-memory > wiki > claude-mem. Higher wins on conflict. Lower promotes by proving itself across sessions.

II

The Ingestion Pipeline

Six channels narrow into one skill, then fan out by domain.

Everything that lands in the wiki arrives through /ingest. One skill, one v4 frontmatter schema, one advisory rubric. There are no backdoors. Channel separation lets each source apply its own triage without branching logic inside the core promoter.

Automated vs manual Sources → raw/ is partly automatic: clippings, meeting notes, and claude-mem observations land without intervention. raw/ → wiki/ is always manual — the queue drains when you run /ingest.

One skill. One schema. One rubric that scores and annotates but never blocks. — the standing rule, stated at steady state

III

The Skill Constellation

Eight skills, with loyalties.

Ninety percent of daily operation runs on three skills: /knowledge at the end of an insight-rich session, /ingest periodically to drain the queue, /reflect and /ship to close. The rest are specialists — /consolidate when observations have accumulated enough to cluster; /vault-lint when you suspect drift; /memory-eval when the rubric itself is under calibration.

IV

The 2026 · 04 · 23 Unlock

Type-aware promotion. Most knowledge earns its keep in one session.

The original promotion_necessity anchor asked a single universal question — is this a recurring pattern across three-or-more sessions? — and encoded Karpathy's wiki model as if it were a general truth. It wasn't. It rejected legitimate single-session decisions, unique references, analyzed incidents, executable howtos, named architectures, and active profiles.

After twenty sessions of process work producing zero net wiki entries, the rubric was relaxed to accept what the user actually generates. Pattern retains the recurrence requirement. The other six types each have their own path.

Why this matters The old rule would have rejected most entries in the wiki as it stands today — including the entries that enabled the rule to be revised. A type-aware anchor lets the architecture be an existence proof of its own criterion.

Advisory, permanently

The rubric scores entries (mem_eval_organize: pass | fail_advisory) and annotates frontmatter — it never blocks, never deletes. Enforce-mode design is parked at memory-architecture/backlog/enforce-mode-trajectory.md with re-open conditions if advisory measurably fails at scale.

The headline

Pattern is the outlier, not the template. Six of the seven types promote cleanly from a single well-formed session. The rubric now accepts the kind of knowledge users actually make.
